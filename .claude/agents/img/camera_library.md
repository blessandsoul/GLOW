# CAMERA LIBRARY — Prompt Interpreter Reference

> Camera techniques organized by category. Use this reference when building prompts that require specific camera work.

---

## 1. Camera Angles (Height & Tilt)

| Angle | Description | Use for |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| Eye Level | Camera at subject's eye height | Neutral, natural perspective. Realism and balance without emotional distortion |
| High Angle | Camera looks down at subject | Making subject appear smaller, weaker, vulnerable, subordinate |
| Low Angle | Camera looks up at subject | Emphasizing power, dominance, importance, intimidation |
| Bird's-Eye View | Camera directly overhead (near 90 degrees) | Layout, geometry, choreography, isolation. Analytical or dramatic |
| Worm's-Eye View | Extreme low angle from ground level looking up | Exaggerating scale and grandeur, making subjects appear monumental |
| Overhead Shot | High vertical angle but not perfectly top-down | Spatial clarity, action geography, stylized compositions |
| Dutch Angle | Camera tilted sideways | Tension, unease, instability, psychological discomfort |

---

## 2. Point of View (Perspective-Based)

| Perspective | Description | Use for |
|-------------|-------------|---------|
| POV (Point of View) | Camera shows what a character sees | Immersion and audience identification with the character |
| Over-the-Shoulder (OTS) | Shot from behind one character facing another | Dialogue scenes, spatial continuity, relationship dynamics |
| Third-Person View | Detached observational perspective | Objective storytelling and narrative clarity |
| Subjective Camera | Fully adopts character's visual perception (may include distortion) | Emotional intensity, altered states, experiential storytelling |
| Objective Camera | Neutral observer perspective | Documentary style, realism, analytical presentation |

---

## 3. Shot Size (Framing Distance)

| Shot | Abbreviation | Description | Use for |
|------|-------------|-------------|---------|
| Extreme Wide Shot | EWS | Very distant, environment dominates frame | Establishing location, scale, isolation |
| Wide Shot | WS | Full subject visible within environment | Body language and context simultaneously |
| Medium Wide Shot | MWS | Knees-up framing | Walking, casual movement, slight context |
| Medium Shot | MS | Framed roughly waist-up | Natural dialogue framing, balanced emotional connection |
| Medium Close-Up | MCU | Chest-up framing | Stronger emotional engagement with slight context |
| Close-Up | CU | Face fills most of frame | Emotional emphasis, intimacy, reaction shots |
| Extreme Close-Up | ECU | Focus on small detail (eyes, hands, object) | Intensity, symbolism, highlighting critical detail |
| Insert Shot | — | Close-up of an object within a scene | Clarifying information (phone screen, letter, key detail) |
| Cutaway | — | Brief shot of something related but not main subject | Pacing, reaction emphasis, hiding edits |
| Two-Shot | — | Two subjects framed together | Relationship dynamics, dialogue, connection |
| Group Shot | — | Three or more subjects in frame | Social context, team dynamics, crowd |
| Establishing Shot | — | Wide view that sets location before closer shots | Scene-setting, geography, time of day |

## 4. Subject Orientation

| Orientation | Description | Use for |
|-------------|-------------|---------|
| Profile Shot | Side view of subject | Dramatic composition, emotional distance, stylized dialogue |
| Front Shot | Directly facing camera | Direct address, interviews, authority, connection |
| Back Shot | Subject seen from behind | Mystery, contemplation, anonymity |
| Three-Quarter Angle | Subject turned slightly toward camera (not full profile) | Depth, flattering composition, natural conversation framing |
| Side Shot | Strong lateral view (often for movement) | Tracking motion, symmetry, graphic composition |
| Ground-Level Shot | Camera placed very low, near floor/ground | Emphasizing scale, movement, dramatic perspective |
| Looking Away | Subject facing away from camera at angle | Contemplation, narrative anticipation, environmental focus |
| Mirror / Dual Angle | Subject and their reflection both visible | Self-awareness, duality, vanity, identity |

---

## 5. Camera Movement Types

| Movement | Description | Use for |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| Static Shot | No camera movement | Stability, focus on performance, formal composition |
| Handheld Shot | Slight natural shake | Realism, urgency, documentary feel |
| Tracking Shot | Camera moves alongside subject | Following action smoothly |
| Dolly Shot | Camera moves forward or backward on tracks | Dramatic emphasis, emotional push-in, spatial exploration |
| Crane Shot | Camera moves vertically through space | Reveals, transitions, epic scale |
| Steadicam Shot | Stabilized smooth movement while walking | Immersive following shots without visible shake |
| Arc Shot | Camera moves in curved path around subject | Dynamic energy, relationship emphasis, dramatic tension |
| Push-In | Gradual move closer to subject | Emotional emphasis, focusing attention |
| Pull-Out | Gradual move away from subject | Distancing, reveal, showing context |
| Jib Up / Jib Down | Small vertical crane movement, controlled rise or lower | Gentle reveal, transition, subtle emphasis |
| Lateral Slide | Smooth horizontal movement parallel to subject | Panoramic scanning, context building, parallel motion |
| Zoom (slow) | Focal length change without camera movement | Gradual emphasis or reveal without spatial change |
| Zoom (snap/crash) | Rapid zoom in or out | Shock, surprise, comedic timing, sudden focus |

## 6. Advanced Camera Angles (Height Variants)

| Angle | Description | Use for |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| Top Shot | Camera directly above subject, clean vertical composition | Strong graphic symmetry, minimalism, control, abstraction |
| Bottom Shot | Camera directly below subject, centered upward | Extreme dominance, monumentality, intimidation |
| Shoulder-Level Shot | Camera aligned with shoulders, slightly below eye level | More physical presence without full low-angle distortion |
| Hip-Level Shot | Camera at waist/hip height | Dynamic movement, casual realism, natural walking shots |
| Knee-Level Shot | Camera near knee height | Emphasizing motion, approach, looming presence |
| Ground POV | Camera on the ground, looking forward or up | Vulnerability, scale exaggeration, stylized realism |
| Table-Level Shot | Camera at surface height of a table | Product shots, food photography, intimate object interaction |
| Rooftop / Balcony Angle | Elevated position looking down at street or courtyard | Urban surveillance, context, cityscape intimacy |

## 7. Psychological / Narrative Angles

| Angle | Description | Use for |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| Oblique Angle | Camera at off-axis diagonal (not just tilt) | Subtle disorientation without obvious Dutch tilt |
| Voyeur Shot | Camera partially obstructed (through objects, frames, windows) | Surveillance, secrecy, intimacy, tension |
| Peeking Shot | Camera looks from behind edge or corner | Suspense, curiosity, hidden observation |
| Surveillance Angle | High, distant, fixed viewpoint (CCTV-style) | Detachment, control, realism, unease |
| Reflection Shot | Subject seen via mirror, glass, or reflection | Duality, self-awareness, visual complexity |
| Confession Angle | Tight close-up, slightly below eye level, subject looking past camera | Vulnerability, intimacy, documentary truth |
| Power Shot | Low angle, wide lens, subject looming large in frame | Authority, threat, overwhelming presence |
| Isolation Shot | Subject small in vast empty space | Loneliness, insignificance, existential weight |
| Claustrophobic Shot | Tight framing, close walls, compressed space around subject | Anxiety, confinement, pressure, tension |
| Confrontation Shot | Dead-center frontal, subject staring directly at camera | Challenge, breaking fourth wall, direct engagement |

## 8. Framing-Based Compositions

| Composition | Description | Use for |
|-------------|-------------|---------|
| Frame-within-a-Frame | Subject framed by doors, windows, or objects | Focus, isolation, visual storytelling |
| Center-Framed Shot | Subject placed exactly in the center | Authority, symmetry, intentional artificiality |
| Rule-of-Thirds Shot | Subject aligned to thirds grid | Natural balance and cinematic composition |
| Negative Space Shot | Large empty area around subject | Loneliness, emphasis, minimalism |
| Silhouette Shot | Subject visible only as dark outline | Mystery, anonymity, dramatic reveal |
| Leading Lines | Environmental lines draw eye toward subject | Direction, depth, visual pull toward focal point |
| Symmetrical Composition | Mirror-balanced left and right halves | Order, formality, Kubrick-style intentionality |
| Asymmetrical Balance | Uneven but visually balanced elements | Natural tension, dynamic interest, editorial feel |
| Foreground Framing | Objects in near foreground create depth layers | Depth, voyeurism, environmental context |
| Golden Ratio / Spiral | Subject placed at golden spiral focal point | Organic beauty, sophisticated composition, fine art |
| Diagonal Composition | Key elements arranged along diagonal line | Energy, movement, dynamic tension |
| Layered Depth | Distinct foreground, midground, background planes | Cinematic depth, visual richness, spatial storytelling |

## 9. Motion + Angle Hybrids

| Movement | Description | Use for |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| Whip Pan | Fast horizontal camera movement | Energy, transitions, time jumps |
| Tilt Shot (Up/Down) | Camera tilts vertically while staying in place | Reveals, scale emphasis |
| Pan Shot (Left/Right) | Camera rotates horizontally | Following action, scene exploration |
| 360-Degree Shot | Camera circles fully around subject | Intensity, climax, immersion |
| Vertigo Effect (Dolly Zoom) | Dolly movement combined with focal zoom | Psychological shock, disorientation, realization |
| Rising Crane + Pan | Crane rises while panning horizontally | Epic reveals, landscape transitions, scene endings |
| Tracking + Low Angle | Moving alongside subject from below | Heroic walk, action approach, power in motion |
| Orbit + Push-In | Circling while gradually getting closer | Building tension, climactic approach, obsessive focus |
| Overhead to Eye Level | Camera descends from top-down to frontal | Scene entry, character introduction, perspective shift |
| Dolly Through | Camera moves through narrow space or opening | Transition between spaces, immersive passage, discovery |
| Parallax Movement | Foreground and background move at different speeds | Depth emphasis, cinematic richness, layered space |

## 10. AI / Stylized / Virtual Camera Angles

| Angle | Description | Use for |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| Floating Camera | Physically impossible smooth movement | Dreamlike, AI-native, surreal visuals |
| Impossible Angle | Camera passes through solid objects or tight gaps | Hyperrealism, spectacle, AI-generated scenes |
| Macro Perspective Shot | Camera behaves as if extremely small | Object-scale storytelling, novelty, talking-object videos |
| Miniature Perspective (Tilt-Shift) | Scene appears like a model | Stylization, abstraction, playful tone |
| God View | High omniscient angle with slow movement | Fate, inevitability, narrative overview |
| Time-Slice / Bullet-Time | Multiple angles captured simultaneously, frozen moment | Action freeze, dramatic emphasis, Matrix-style spectacle |
| Infinite Zoom | Continuous zoom that passes through scales (macro to cosmic) | Mind-bending transitions, fractal exploration, scale play |
| Split-Screen POV | Frame divided showing multiple simultaneous perspectives | Parallel action, comparison, multiple viewpoints |
| Phantom Ride | Camera mounted at front of moving vehicle or object | Immersive travel, first-person journey, forward momentum |
| Drone Reveal | Ascending aerial pullback revealing landscape or context | Epic scale reveal, establishing grandeur, transition to wide |
| Through-Surface Shot | Camera passes through water, glass, or translucent surface | Transition between worlds, immersive passage, surreal shift |

## 11. Lens Focal Length Guide

| Focal Length | Type | Visual Effect | Best for |
|-------------|------|---------------|----------|
| 14-24mm | Ultra-wide | Dramatic distortion, vast space, curved lines at edges | Architecture, landscapes, immersive environments |
| 24-35mm | Wide | Slight expansion of space, environmental context visible | Street photography, interiors, environmental portraits |
| 35-50mm | Standard | Closest to human eye perception, natural proportions | Documentary, lifestyle, general purpose |
| 50mm | Normal | Zero distortion, classic look, natural perspective | Portraits, product, everyday realism |
| 85mm | Short telephoto | Flattering compression, beautiful background blur | Portraits, beauty, character close-ups |
| 100-135mm | Medium telephoto | Strong compression, isolated subject, creamy bokeh | Headshots, product detail, intimate moments |
| 200mm+ | Long telephoto | Extreme compression, stacked planes, distant subject | Sports, wildlife, compressed cityscapes |
| Macro lens | Specialty | 1:1 reproduction, extreme detail at close range | Jewelry, insects, textures, small objects |
| Fisheye | Specialty | 180-degree spherical distortion, circular or full-frame | Creative, action sports, immersive, surreal |
| Tilt-shift | Specialty | Selective focus plane, miniature effect | Architecture, miniature illusion, creative focus |
