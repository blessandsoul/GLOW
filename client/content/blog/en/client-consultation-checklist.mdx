<h2 id="why-consultations-matter">Why Consultations Matter More Than Technique</h2>

<p>You can be the most technically skilled lash artist in your city and still lose clients if your consultations are weak. The consultation is where trust is built, expectations are set, and potential problems are identified before they become real issues. A thorough 10-15 minute consultation prevents bad reviews, allergic reactions, and the dreaded "this isn't what I wanted" conversation.</p>

<p>This checklist covers everything you should discuss, document, and assess before picking up your tweezers.</p>

<h2 id="health-and-medical-screening">Health and Medical Screening</h2>

<h3 id="allergies-and-sensitivities">Allergies and Sensitivities</h3>

<p>Ask directly: "Have you ever had an allergic reaction to eyelash extensions, adhesive, or any beauty product applied near your eyes?" Document the answer regardless. First-time clients with no history of allergies still deserve a patch test — apply 5-10 extensions to each eye and wait 24-48 hours before proceeding with a full set.</p>

<p>Also ask about latex allergies (some eye pads contain latex), sensitivity to formaldehyde (present in some adhesives), and general skin conditions like eczema or psoriasis around the eye area.</p>

<h3 id="eye-conditions">Eye Conditions</h3>

<p>Screen for active eye conditions that contraindicate lash extensions:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Blepharitis:</strong> Inflammation of the eyelids. Extensions can worsen this condition. Refer the client to an ophthalmologist first.</li>
<li><strong>Conjunctivitis:</strong> Never apply extensions during an active eye infection. Reschedule once the infection has fully cleared.</li>
<li><strong>Styes:</strong> Active styes near the lash line make safe application impossible.</li>
<li><strong>Dry eye syndrome:</strong> Extensions can exacerbate dryness. Discuss this with the client and consider lighter sets.</li>
<li><strong>Recent eye surgery:</strong> Wait at least 6 months after LASIK, cataract surgery, or any procedure involving the eye area.</li>
</ul>

<h3 id="medications">Medications and Treatments</h3>

<p>Certain medications affect lash retention. Thyroid medications, retinoids (Accutane), and some hormonal treatments can cause lashes to shed faster or grow more slowly. This does not mean you cannot apply extensions — it means you should set realistic expectations about retention and fill frequency.</p>

<p>Clients undergoing chemotherapy should not receive extensions. Their natural lashes are fragile, and the immune suppression increases infection risk.</p>

<h2 id="natural-lash-assessment">Natural Lash Assessment</h2>

<h3 id="lash-health-check">Lash Health Check</h3>

<p>Before discussing styles, examine the natural lashes under magnification. Look for:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Length:</strong> Short natural lashes (under 7mm) limit extension length options. A general rule is that extensions should not exceed 2-3mm beyond the natural lash length.</li>
<li><strong>Thickness:</strong> Fine, wispy natural lashes cannot support heavy fans. Scale down volume accordingly.</li>
<li><strong>Density:</strong> Sparse natural lashes with visible gaps along the lash line limit how full the final result can look. Be honest about this.</li>
<li><strong>Growth direction:</strong> Lashes that grow straight down or at unusual angles require specific curl types (L or L+ curls) to achieve a lifted look.</li>
<li><strong>Damage:</strong> Look for broken, kinked, or unusually short lashes that suggest damage from previous extensions, lash curlers, or rubbing. Recommend a recovery period if damage is significant.</li>
</ul>

<h3 id="growth-cycle-stage">Growth Cycle Considerations</h3>

<p>Explain to clients that lashes go through growth cycles, and not all lashes are at the same stage. Baby lashes (anagen phase) are shorter and thinner — avoid overloading them with heavy extensions. Mature lashes (catagen/telogen phase) will shed naturally within weeks regardless of how well you apply extensions. This is normal, not a sign of poor work.</p>

<h2 id="style-consultation">Style Consultation</h2>

<h3 id="understanding-client-goals">Understanding What the Client Actually Wants</h3>

<p>Ask open-ended questions rather than jumping straight to technical options:</p>

<ul>
<li>"What do you want your lashes to do for you?" — This reveals whether they want natural enhancement or dramatic impact.</li>
<li>"Do you wear eye makeup daily?" — Heavy eyeshadow users may prefer a more dramatic lash set since they are already comfortable with bold eye looks.</li>
<li>"What is your morning routine like?" — Low-maintenance clients benefit from classic or light volume sets that require less aftercare.</li>
<li>"Have you had extensions before? What did you like or dislike?" — Past experience gives you the clearest direction.</li>
</ul>

<h3 id="reference-photos">Using Reference Photos Wisely</h3>

<p>Encourage clients to bring reference photos, but manage expectations carefully. Explain that results depend on their natural lash characteristics, eye shape, and the specific products used. A photo of a 16D mega volume set on someone with naturally thick, dense lashes will not look the same on a client with sparse, fine lashes.</p>

<p>Keep your own portfolio organized by style, eye shape, and technique so you can show realistic examples of what you can achieve on similar lash types. <h3 id="eye-shape-analysis">Eye Shape Analysis</h3>

<p>Assess the client's eye shape and recommend a mapping style that enhances their features:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Almond eyes:</strong> The most versatile shape. Most mapping styles work well. Cat eye and natural maps are particularly flattering.</li>
<li><strong>Round eyes:</strong> Cat eye mapping elongates round eyes beautifully. Avoid doll eye maps that emphasize roundness further.</li>
<li><strong>Hooded eyes:</strong> Use stronger curls (CC or D) so extensions are visible when eyes are open. Open eye mapping with longer center lashes creates a lifting effect.</li>
<li><strong>Downturned eyes:</strong> Avoid long outer corners that exaggerate the downward angle. Squirrel or natural mapping lifts the outer edge.</li>
<li><strong>Close-set eyes:</strong> Cat eye mapping with longer outer extensions creates the illusion of wider spacing.</li>
<li><strong>Wide-set eyes:</strong> Doll eye mapping with emphasis on the center draws eyes closer together visually.</li>
</ul>

<h2 id="expectation-management">Setting Realistic Expectations</h2>

<h3 id="retention-timeline">Retention and Fill Schedule</h3>

<p>Be explicit about the timeline. A full set lasts 3-4 weeks before a fill is needed. Most clients lose 40-50% of extensions by week three due to the natural lash growth cycle. Fills are not optional maintenance — they are essential for keeping the set looking full and preventing an uneven, grown-out appearance.</p>

<h3 id="lifestyle-factors">Lifestyle Factors That Affect Retention</h3>

<p>Discuss honestly: oil-based skincare products dissolve adhesive, swimming in chlorinated water weakens bonds, sleeping face-down crushes extensions, and touching or rubbing eyes accelerates shedding. Clients who are not willing to adjust these habits will experience poor retention regardless of how perfectly you apply the set.</p>

<h3 id="pricing-transparency">Pricing Transparency</h3>

<p>State your pricing clearly during the consultation, not after the service. Include the cost of the initial full set, fills at different intervals (2-week fill vs. 3-week fill), removal, and any add-ons like colored lashes or bottom lash extensions. Clients who understand the ongoing investment are less likely to skip fills or seek cheaper alternatives that damage their lashes.</p>

<h2 id="consent-and-documentation">Consent and Documentation</h2>

<h3 id="consent-form">Consent Form Essentials</h3>

<p>Every client should sign a consent form before their first appointment. Include:</p>

<ul>
<li>Acknowledgment of potential risks (allergic reaction, irritation, natural lash damage if aftercare is not followed)</li>
<li>Medical history declaration</li>
<li>Aftercare agreement</li>
<li>Photo consent (separate from the service consent)</li>
<li>Cancellation and refund policy</li>
</ul>

<h3 id="before-photos">Taking Before Photos</h3>

<p>Photograph the natural lashes before every appointment — full set and fills. This protects you if a client claims their natural lashes were damaged, and it creates a visual record of their lash health over time. Front-facing and side-profile shots capture the most useful information.</p>

<h2 id="aftercare-education">Aftercare Education</h2>

<p>Do not assume clients know how to care for their extensions. Walk through each point:</p>

<ul>
<li>Avoid water for the first 24 hours (some adhesives need 48 hours for full cure).</li>
<li>Cleanse lashes daily with a dedicated foaming lash cleanser.</li>
<li>Brush gently with a clean spoolie each morning.</li>
<li>Avoid oil-based products near the eyes.</li>
<li>Do not pull, pick, or rub extensions.</li>
<li>Sleep on your back or on a silk pillowcase.</li>
</ul>

<p>Provide a printed or digital aftercare card so they can reference it later. Following up with a message 24-48 hours after the appointment asking how their lashes feel shows professionalism and builds loyalty.</p>

<h2 id="consultation-builds-business">The Consultation Builds Your Business</h2>

<p>A thorough consultation does more than protect you legally and clinically. It positions you as a knowledgeable professional who cares about outcomes, not just revenue. Clients who feel heard and educated during consultation become loyal, long-term clients who refer their friends. In a market flooded with lash artists competing on price, your consultation process is what sets you apart.</p>
